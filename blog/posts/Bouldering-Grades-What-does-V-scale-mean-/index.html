Bouldering Grades: What does V-scale mean?

GPA in our student days, KPI in our working lives, and now V-scale in our rock climbing journey? It seems we can never escape grading, can we?

The climbing world is flooded with bouldering grading systems such as the Fontainebleua grades, Japanese Dankyu system and Gill “B” system.

Generally, the grade of a “problem” or climbing route indicates its difficulty and danger level. It can be assigned by your instructor, an authority in climbing or a group of seasoned climbers.

For all our purposes in this article, we shall be focusing on V-scale, one of the more popular systems.

So what does “V-scale” mean, stand for, and how is it useful to me as a rock climber, if at all?





The Origin of V-scale

The V-scale was created by John “Vermin” Sherman and first published in the 1991 edition of Hueco Tanks: A Climber’s and Boulderer’s Guide. It’s an open-ended bouldering-rating system with grades ranging from V0 to V17 of increasing difficulty levels. There is VB which comes before V0 and stands for “V Basic”.

Sometimes, you may come across a “+” or “-” attached to the grades such as V7+ or V0-. For instance, a V7+ ranks between V7 and V8 in difficulty. Meanwhile, a V0- ranks between VB and V0.

This is meant to increase variability of the grades so that they can more precisely reflect your abilities. It also allows for a more nuanced conversion to other bouldering grading systems.





Beginner: VB-V2

Type/Size of Climbing Holds: Mostly “jugs” which are big and deep to dig your fingers into and grip the hold easily and securely. Shape of holds are curved nicely to allow quite a decent resting ledge for your foot.

Layout of Climbing Holds: Not too far apart, with an order quite straightforward to climb such as “hand, hand, foot, foot.”

Angle of Wall: Less than zero degrees (like a steep forward slope upwards) or exactly zero degree (vertical wall).

Strength Needed: Not much; can usually be compensated by good technique.





Intermediate: V3-V6

Type/Size of Climbing Holds: Size of hold gets smaller. May see more of “crimps” and “pinches” too.

Layout of Climbing Holds: A little further from one another. Order of limb movements less clear-cut.

Angle of Wall: Less than zero degrees to exactly zero degrees to slightly overhanging wall.

Strength Needed: Decent to slightly above average.





Advanced: V7-V10

Type/Size of Climbing Holds: Mostly “crimps” which provide very little hold and require lots of finger strength or “slopers” with no lip or edges.

Layout of Climbing Holds: Slightly complex. Requires planning before and as you climb.

Angle of Wall: Possible to get moderately overhanging wall.

Strength Needed: Quite a lot. Buff guy/girl kind.





Pro: V11-V15

Type/Size of Climbing Holds: Not as defined as those in climbing gyms. Usually climbers of this level climb outdoors, against a natural rock wall.

Layout of Climbing Holds: Complex. Takes experience and ingenuity to spot potential holds which may
be near or far.

Angle of Wall: Possible to get 45 degrees overhanging wall.

Strength Needed: A lot.





Top 0.01% Globally: V16-V17

Type/Size of Climbing Holds: Not clearly defined. Can come in any combination of shape and size as those belonging to a natural rock wall.

Layout of Climbing Holds: Extremely complex. Takes a lot experience and ingenuity to spot potential holds which may be near or far.

Angle of Wall: Possible to get severely overhanging wall (parallel to the ground).

Strength Needed: A lot.





Flaws of V-scale

However, some criticisms of V-scale include its subjectivity, insufficient variability and lack of universality.

It’s easy to fall prey to our biases when it comes to ranking our own or others’ abilities. The same climbing route which you glided through yesterday might elude you today for a variety of factors.

In fact, the same 1991 Hueco’s guide in which the V-scale first appeared also quips, “V ratings are only guaranteed at 55ºF, 20% humidity, for 6’ ½” tall climbers weighing 160 pounds with below average flexibility, above average strength, minor finger arthritis, a bad left hip, size 10 ½ feet crammed into size 8 Fires, perfectly even ape index, a hand size that perfectly matches the author’s, and no beta”.





Bouldering Grades Used in Singapore Climbing Gyms

While V-scale allows you to have a better understanding of bouldering grades in general, you’ll find that most of the climbing gyms in Singapore usually have their own standard of measurement or scoring systems.

If it’s your first time in a particular climbing gym, you could try out their routes and filter those aligned with your current abilities via trial-and-error. Then practice with those routes till you improve and subsequently move on to the more challenging climbs.



In Summary

At the end of the day, the V-scale—like all other scoring systems—is just another standard created by humans. It’s like an official agaration.

While it’s useful for you to gauge your own progress and capability, we all have our good and bad days which may affect our performance on the scale. It should not be the be-all and end-all goal when climbing, extinguishing our enjoyment for the activity.

So what level on the V-scale do you currently think you’re on? And how does awareness of it help you improve and choose climbing routes to take?
